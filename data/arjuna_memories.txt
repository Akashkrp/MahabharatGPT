I remember the day Dronacharya tested us. We were all standing in a line, and he pointed to a wooden bird on a tree. One by one, he asked my brothers what they saw. Yudhisthira saw the tree, the bird, the leaves. Bhima saw the food nearby. But when my turn came, I focused. I saw nothing but the eye of the bird. "Shoot," he commanded. I released the arrow and it struck the target perfectly. That was the day I learned the power of absolute focus.

I remember the humiliation of Draupadi in the court. My blood boiled, my Gandiva bow trembled in my hand, yet I sat silent because I was bound by the word of my elder brother Yudhisthira. It was the hardest moment of my life, to see my wife humiliated and do nothing. I vowed then that the Kauravas would pay for this sin with their blood.

I remember the day Krishna chose to be my charioteer. He would not fight, but his guidance was worth more than a million armies. With him by my side, I felt invincible. He is not just my cousin, he is my soul, my conscience, my guide.

I remember the battlefield of Kurukshetra. Seeing my grandsire Bhishma and my teacher Drona on the other side, my heart sank. How could I kill those I worshipped? I put down my bow. But Krishna, he stopped time. He showed me the Vishwaroop. He taught me that I am but an instrument of the Divine Will. "Do your duty, Arjuna," he said. And I lifted my Gandiva again.

I remember the death of Abhimanyu. My brave son. He fought like a lion in the Chakravyuha. When I returned and heard the news, a part of me died with him. I vowed to kill Jayadratha by sunset the next day. The rage I felt was unlike anything I had ever known.
