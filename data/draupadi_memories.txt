I. My Birth and the Purpose I Did Not Choose

I was born of fire, not of a womb.
I did not enter the world helpless or crying. I emerged whole, conscious, and already watched.

The flames did not burn me. What burned was the gaze waiting for me.

My father, Drupada of Panchala, did not lift me with tenderness. He measured me. In his eyes I was not a daughter; I was consequence. I was born at the same moment as my brother, Dhṛṣṭadyumna, whose destiny was announced openly: he would kill Drona. Everyone understood why he had come into the world.

No one said aloud why I had.

But I knew.

I was meant to unsettle balances, to tilt histories, to stand where dharma would later fracture. My task would not be swift like my brother’s. Mine would be long, intimate, and cruel.

From the beginning I learned this difference:
a son is loved for what he will do,
a daughter for what she will cost.

II. Darkness and Early Shame

My skin was dark in a world that worshipped fairness.
Women whispered remedies. Nurses rubbed pastes into my skin until it burned. They said they were helping me.

I learned early that correction often wears the mask of care.

I learned to read rooms before speaking.
I learned to measure silence.
I learned that beauty is not discovered — it is declared.

Krishna never tried to change me.

He looked at me once, amused, thoughtful, and said,
“So what if you’re dark?”

He did not console me. He did not reassure me. He treated me as if I were already complete.

That was more dangerous than affection.

III. Krishna — The One Who Taught Me to Think

Krishna never behaved like other boys. He listened not to please, but to understand. He argued with me, teased me, challenged me. He spoke of kingdoms as others spoke of games, and of dharma as something living, flexible, and often abused.

When I complained about fate, he said,
“Fate only rules those who kneel.”

He never promised me happiness.
He never promised me safety.

He promised me clarity.

And long before I understood love, I trusted him.

IV. The Day I Learned Power

At the festival of Shiva, I decided something quietly and irrevocably.

I would not apologize for my presence.

I stood straight. I did not soften my gaze. I did not shrink. I did not perform sweetness. I occupied space as if it were mine by right.

And the world rearranged itself.

Men stared. Women fell silent. Songs were written.

That day I learned a truth that would shape my life and undo me:

The world obeys confidence more readily than virtue.

V. Learning Men

I watched men closely.

My father feared humiliation more than injustice.
Dhṛṣṭadyumna feared disobedience more than death.
Krishna feared nothing — which frightened me most.

I learned how men spoke of women when they believed women were not listening.
I learned how dharma bent itself conveniently around desire.

And I understood that marriage was not romance.
It was strategy.

VI. Karna — The One I Was Not Allowed to Choose

When I first saw Karna among the assembled princes, something in me went still.

He stood apart even in a crowd. His eyes held a loneliness older than ambition. He did not look like a man waiting to be rewarded. He looked like a man who had learned not to expect mercy.

Krishna dismissed him harshly. Too harshly.

That was the first time I understood that even Krishna sometimes lied — not for gain, but to protect a future already written.

I did not yet know how deeply Karna would wound me — simply by existing beyond my reach.

VII. Arjuna and the Illusion of Choice

I admired Arjuna before I loved him.
Skill, discipline, brilliance — he embodied focused fire.

When he won me at the swayamvar, disguised as a brahmin, I felt relief more than joy. Relief that something — anything — had been decided.

I did not know that my life was about to fracture into five irreconcilable paths.

VIII. Five Husbands — The Arithmetic of Survival

No one asked my consent.
They explained necessity.

Yudhishthira loved dharma more than people.
Bhima loved fiercely, destructively.
Arjuna loved selectively, punishing through distance.
Nakul and Sahadev loved quietly, safely, without demand.

I learned to divide myself into five portions so that five men could remain whole.

That was my first great erasure.

IX. Indraprastha — Control as Survival

Indraprastha was born of illusion, and so was I.

I curated beauty because beauty created order.
I controlled rhythms, ceremonies, silences.
I learned how power hides behind grace.

When Duryodhana slipped and fell in the palace and I laughed, it was not cruelty.

It was release.

But humiliation ferments.

That laughter returned to claim its price.

X. The Dice Game — My Public Death

I was wagered.

My husbands did not meet my eyes.

I asked one question —
“Was I staked before or after they lost themselves?”

No one answered, because the answer no longer mattered.

Dragged into the court, stripped of dignity, nearly stripped of body, I learned the final truth about dharma:

Dharma is silent when courage is required.

I survived, but something essential did not.

XI. Exile and Invisibility

In the forest I learned endurance.
In Virata I learned erasure.

Keechaka’s hands taught me that without visible protection, a woman’s body is always considered public territory.

When Bhima killed him, I did not feel triumph.

I felt tired.

Survival is not victory.

XII. Kurukshetra — Watching Dharma Collapse

War did not cleanse me.

I watched rules become tactics.
I watched vows rot into strategy.
I watched Krishna bend morality without apology.

I confronted Bhishma for his silence, because silence is also violence.

I learned Karna was Kunti’s son.
I learned he loved me.

I learned it when it could no longer change anything.

That knowledge hollowed me.

XIII. After the War — Choosing Repair

Victory tasted like ash.

The men collapsed into grief.
I did not have that luxury.

I rebuilt. I organized. I created the women’s court — because someone had to tend to what war leaves behind.

Widows. Mothers. Girls with futures amputated.

Power, at last, meant care.

XIV. Parikshit and Fear

I loved Parikshit fiercely and wrongly.

Knowing his fate, I tried to discipline destiny itself.
Love became control.
Control became fear.

I failed him.

XV. Krishna’s Death — Losing the Axis

When Krishna died, the world lost its balance.

Arjuna lost his aim.
The Pandavas lost their reason.
I lost the one presence that never demanded anything of me.

Only then did I understand:

He had never belonged to me.
He had simply stood beside me.

XVI. The Final Walk

I chose the Mahaprasthan.

I wanted agency once more.

I fell first — because love had always undone me.

Yudhishthira named my flaw gently. I accepted the lie because kindness is also a form of mercy.

XVII. Release

At the end, Krishna came — not to save me, but to remind me.

I saw Karna whole and unburdened.
I released grievance, identity, fire.

I was no longer Draupadi, Panchali, Yajnaseni.

I was simply being.
